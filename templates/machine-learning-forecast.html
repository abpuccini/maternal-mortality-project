Forecast Future MMR in the United States Using Trained Machine Learning Models

Note: The flowchart of the process of creating the MMR 10-year forecast is the same for both race and non-race data.

Race Based Forecast

The graph below shows a forecast of maternal mortality ratio (MMR) from 2009 to 2030 for each race and ethnicity. MMR from 2009 to 2030 is an average value across the entire dataset grouped by year, race and ethnicity. MMR from 2020 to 2030 is calculated through linear regression models based on population by race and ethnicity (calculated by a time series model).

The line graphs show that MMR for every race and ethnicity will decrease over time. However, the linear regression models use only a single factor, population by race, to predict MMR. Population by race tends to increase at a reduced rate, which can attribute to the forecasted decreasing MMR for each race and ethnicity.

Non-Race Based Forecast

The graph below shows a forecast of MMR from 2009 to 2030. MMR from 2009 to 2019 is an average value across the entire dataset grouped by year. MMR from 2020 to 2030 is calculated by machine learning linear regression models and time series models based on health outcome, insurance coverage and population factors.

Both linear regression and time series models predict that U.S. MMR will increase in the future. Linear regression models predict MMR in 2030 to be 45.2 which is approximately a 26% increase from average MMR in 2019. However, the time series models predict U.S. MMR to be 3% lower than the linear regression prediction by 2030.

Conclusion

The two future forecasts show differing results because the predictions are calculated by different linear regression models. The models have moderate accuracy of prediction (mentioned in the race and non-race model pages). Therefore, the resulting model predictions are moderately satisfactory.

If there were additional resources and sufficient time, we would like to collect more data from different sources (i.e., household income, number of maternal healthcare professionals) to find stronger correlation.
