Non-Race Based Machine Learning Models

Understand how Machine Learning Models Fit Race-Exclusive US MMR Data

Overview

After visualizing maternal mortality ratio (MMR) data from the United States and seeing disparities among states in insurance coverage and health measures, we decided to apply machine learning to the problem. It was clear from initial research that racial and ethnic disparities existed, so we collected two datasets: one that utilized MMR data stratified by race, and another that included MMR data for all races and also included insurance status and 26 different health measures. We then trained and tested a variety of models before choosing the top-performing models for each subset of data.

Linear Regression Model

We chose linear regression analysis, which is supervised machine learning, to create a model that examines the impact of various factors on maternal mortality ratio (MMR) irrespective of race. We determined correlations using linear regression analyses which indicated both positive and negative relationships.

Based upon an initial heatmap which included all features, a second, more focused heatmap was developed. In the focused heatmap pictured above, the analyses indicated that diabetes and premature death had the strongest positive correlations. Other important correlations included positive relationships with physical inactivity, obesity and low birth weight. Interestingly, medicare coverage also had a moderately strong correlation with MMR. High health status, which is the percentage of women who reported that their health is very good or excellent, had the strongest negative correlation in addition to higher weighted sums of all determinants and health outcomes from the national average. Dental visits also had a moderately strong negative correlation with MMR.

The above figure graphically indicates the degree of positive and negative correlation that our linear model found between the most significant features and MMR. We applied another linear regression model with non-race based data and included only factors that had a correlation greater than 0.20, which suggest a moderate or greater association. But removing insignificant variables did not improve R-squared values for any other linear regression models.

All features except birth and death were compared against MMR. R-squared for all the features was 0.54, which suggests that together the features only moderately predict the MMR outcome. The training and the test scores for the linear regression were 0.54 and 0.36 respectively, which are moderately confident fits.

Polynomial Regression Model

Further reduction in features in our linear models resulted in a slightly lowered R-squared (0.43), and by examining the features with the most positive and negative correlation with MMR, depicted in the figure below, it was determined that the data was non-linear. So, a polynomial regression was applied and the features were converted into polynomial feature at degree 2. Plotting the actual MMR, the linear regression MMR and polynomial fit MMR demonstrated that the polynomial regression modeled the MMR relationship with the variables better than the linear regression model.

Time Series Linear Model

The dataset was grouped by year and the average annual MMR was calculated for 2009 to 2019 and then used in the time-series forecast to calculate the average predicted rates. A regression was performed by year and an R-squared of 0.74 was observed. Maternal mortality rate predictions were then carried out for 2020 to 2030. Maternal mortality rates increased slowly from 2009 to 2019, and based on the forecast, rates continue to increase at the same pace until 2030. Healthy People 2030’s goal for maternal mortality rate is to reduce to 15.7 maternal deaths per 100,000 births, however our model suggests that in this time MMR will actually increase by 25% to approximately 44.

Clearly, the forecast depends on the variables continuing their current trend for the next 10 years. But these trends can change and alter the trajectory of maternal mortality rates. If rates of diabetes, which had the strongest correlation with MMR, were to decrease or even just maintain due to effective interventions (e.g., change in dietary habits), then it is probable that the forecasted MMR would not increase as much from 2020 to 2030. This also applies to changes in obesity rates, physical inactivity, health status of women, and other factors like dental visits, all of which could drastically impact MMR in the years to come.

Neural Network/Deep Learning Model

Given the nature of the data pulled, it was always more likely that a linear regression was the better fit for the data, but we wanted to attempt to model a neural network as well and see if anything surprising could be learned. A similar process was used to prepare the data as with the linear regressions, then a base sequential model was created, and using KerasRegressor and setting loss to mean squared error, various fits were explored. No matter the data portion that was input, and any number of hidden layers added, the mean squared error never was seen below 1,000, which was a much worse outcome than the linear models already created.
