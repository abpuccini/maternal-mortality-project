Race Based Machine Learning Models

Understand how Machine Learning Models Fit Race-Inclusive US MMR Data

Overview

After visualizing maternal mortality ratio (MMR) data from the United States and seeing disparities among states in insurance coverage and health measures, we decided to apply machine learning to the problem. It was clear from initial research that racial and ethnic disparities existed, so we collected two datasets: one that utilized MMR data stratified by race, and another that included MMR data for all races and also included insurance status and 26 different health measures. We then trained and tested a variety of models before choosing the top-performing models for each subset of data.

Linear Regression Model

For our dataset stratified by race and ethnicity, we built a linear regression model.

We collected publicly available United States mortality data from the CDC Wonder site, selecting for ICD codes A34 (Obstetrical tetanus) and O00 to O99 (Chapter XV Pregnancy, childbirth, and the puerperium), which captures maternal deaths owing to obstetrical tetanus, maternal deaths up to 42 days after delivery, and late maternal deaths (up to a year following the termination of a pregnancy). One limitation of publicly available mortality data from CDC Wonder site is that the API suppresses data points with death counts of nine or fewer. As a result, only four racial and ethnic groups are represented in our dataset, and some groups are missing data for some years in our range of 2009-2019.

Our data shows the wide racial and ethnic gaps in MMR in the United States. MMR was at least twice as high for Black or African American women compared to other groups.

In the heatmap below, we can see strong positive correlations (likely to indicate higher MMR) for Black or African American women, and negative correlations (likely to indicate lower MMR) for White women.

We knew from earlier exploratory analysis that our data had outliers. We experimented with scaling our data using StandardScaler, best for outliers, and fit our model again, but the resulting R-squared score was slightly lower. Our highest scoring linear regression model with data stratified by race was with non-scaled data. This model used each of our race and hispanic origin categories and population data stratified by race. Our R-squared scores for this model were 0.56 on our testing data and were 0.59 on our training data.

Logistic Regression Model

After running linear regression models, we tried logistic regression, converting our y-value (MMR) to categorical.

We binned our MMR data stratified by race into three categories:

  • Low (MMR less than or equal to 20)
  • Medium (MMR greater than 20 and less than or equal to 50)
  • High (MMR greater than 50)

Our R-squared scores for this model were 0.59 on our testing data and were 0.75 on our training data. The confusion matrix shows that identifying MMR data points as "medium" risk was most successful, followed by appropriately identifying data points as "high" risk.

Summary

The CDC estimates that 60% of maternal deaths in the U.S. are preventable (NMMRC, 2018). And the Pregnancy Mortality Surveillance System reports that the variability in the risk of death by race and ethnicity may be due to several factors including access to care, quality of care, prevalence of chronic diseases, structural racism, and implicit biases (PMSS, 2021).

Our research showed that there are wide disparities in MMR by race and ethnicity, and we were interested in looking closely at possible factors that could be contributing to that disparity. We moved forward with our dataset and models that included features such as access to care.

We encourage visitors of our website to see an application of our race based machine learning models on the 10-Year US MMR Forecast page.
