US MMR Data

The National Center for Health Statistics data on maternal deaths in the United States from 2009 to 2019 was collected from the CDC Wonder site.

Total maternal mortality included maternal deaths owing to obstetrical tetanus, maternal deaths up to 42 days after delivery, as well as late maternal deaths (the death of a woman from direct or indirect obstetric causes, more than 42 days but less than one year after termination of pregnancy).

The maternal mortality ratio was calculated as the number of maternal deaths per 100,000 live births using the National Center for Health Statistics natality data collected from the CDC Wonder site.

Several states did not provide data on maternal deaths between 2009 and 2019. Between 2003 and 2017, states were incrementally implementing a pregnancy status checkbox on death certificates which affected the comparability of data between states. By 2017, all states had implemented a checkbox.

States With No Medicaid Expansion

As of 2017, Medicaid coverage was responsible for financing 43% of U.S. births. A study published in February 2020 found that Medicaid expansion through the Affordable Care Act was significantly associated with lower maternal mortality when compared to states that had not expanded medicaid.

As of December 2020, 12 states in the U.S. have no ACA Medicaid Expansion: Alabama, Florida, Georgia, Kansas, Mississippi, North Carolina, South Carolina, South Dakota, Tennessee, Texas, Wisconsin and Wyoming.

Summary

The U.S. has the highest and most concerning maternal mortality ratio among high-income nations. Within the U.S. there is a disparity in maternal mortality ratio among states, and access to insurance and medicaid coverage is an important factor. State decisions affect women's access to prenatal and postpartum care and affect the resulting outcomes of pregnancy.
